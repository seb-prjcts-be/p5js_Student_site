What is AI?

AI stands for Artificial Intelligence. It's software that recognises patterns in large amounts of data and makes predictions or decisions based on those patterns.

Important: AI doesn't think like a human. It recognises statistical patterns. When an AI chatbot gives an answer, it calculates word by word what the most likely continuation is - based on billions of texts it was trained on.

What AI can do

  • Write, summarise and translate text
  • Generate and explain code
  • Recognise and generate images
  • Find patterns in large datasets
  • Create music and sound

What AI cannot do

  • Truly understand what it says - it simulates understanding
  • Think originally - it combines existing patterns
  • Guarantee facts - it can produce convincing nonsense (hallucinations)
  • Know your intention - it guesses based on your words

Educational AI

AI tools that help you learn are called educational AI. These are not replacements for your own thinking - they are tools you can use to understand faster, investigate deeper and experiment more creatively.

Think of:

  • A chatbot that helps you understand a difficult concept
  • A tool that explains your code, line by line
  • An assistant that brainstorms with you on your creative project

The difference with just "using ChatGPT"? With educational AI, the goal is that you learn something - not that the AI takes over the work.

Thinking critically about AI

AI is powerful, but not infallible. A critical attitude is essential:

Always verify

AI models can mix up facts or completely fabricate them. This is called hallucinating. The model produces text that sounds convincing but is factually incorrect. Always check whether information is correct - especially with specific names, dates or technical details.

Understand the limitations

Every AI model has a knowledge cutoff (cutoff date): anything that happened after a certain date, it doesn't know. Additionally, every model has blind spots - topics it's less well-informed about.

Be aware of bias

AI learns from existing data. If that data is skewed - too many texts from one culture, one language, one perspective - then the model reflects that skew. This is not a conscious choice by the AI, but a property of the training data.

Getting started

The best way to get to know AI is by trying it. Open one of these tools and ask a question you genuinely want answered:

  • ChatGPT - chat.openai.com
  • Gemini - gemini.google.com
  • Claude - claude.ai

Try asking the same question to different models. Compare the answers: which model gives the most useful response? Which one hallucinates? Which one fits best with your way of thinking?
