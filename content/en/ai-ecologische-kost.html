Students often ask: "Is AI bad for the environment?" That's a very valid question. AI does have an ecological cost - but the story is more nuanced than it might seem at first glance.

Training vs. usage

The biggest environmental impact of AI lies in training a model - not in daily usage.

What is training?

When an AI model is created, computers must process enormous amounts of data to learn patterns. This happens on large clusters of specialized chips (GPUs) in data centers, which sometimes run continuously for weeks or months.

Training a large model:
→ thousands of GPUs
→ running for weeks or months
→ costs a lot of electricity and cooling

But - training is a one-time event

After training, millions of people use the same model. The training cost is thus spread across an enormous number of conversations. The energy per user becomes very small.

  • Training: happens occasionally, costs a lot of energy
  • Usage: daily, by millions of people, relatively little energy per query

How much energy does an AI query cost?

An AI conversation costs energy, but less than most people think. Here's a comparison:

Activity Estimated energy
Google search ~0.3 Wh
One AI query ~1–5 Wh
AI conversation (~20 messages) ~20–100 Wh
1 hour of Netflix streaming ~80–100 Wh
Cup of coffee (full chain) ~100–200 Wh
Boiling water ~1000 Wh

The surprising result: a cup of coffee costs as much or even more energy than a complete AI conversation.

The energy cost of coffee doesn't come from brewing it, but from the production chain of the bean: agriculture, water, transportation across continents, roasting, and grinding. Research from University College London, among others, estimates this at 100–200 Wh per cup.

Data centers are going green

Many AI data centers already run on renewable energy:

  • Hydropower, wind energy, and solar energy
  • Companies like Google, Microsoft, and OpenAI invest heavily in green power
  • New AI chips become more efficient per computation every year

The efficiency of AI hardware improves approximately 2x every 1–1.5 years - faster than the classic Moore's Law. The same AI training may cost a fraction of the current energy in just a few years.

Some data centers use their waste heat to warm neighborhoods. In Scandinavia and Finland, servers are connected to district heating - the heat from AI computations doesn't go to waste but heats homes.

The real question: what do we use AI for?

The most important ecological question isn't really how much energy AI uses, but what we use it for.

AI can cost energy, but also save energy:

  • Optimizing energy consumption in buildings
  • Making transport and logistics more efficient
  • Reducing material usage in production
  • Accelerating scientific research (climate, medicine, materials)

A good frame of reference:

"AI is like electricity in the 20th century. It costs energy, but the question is: are we using it for useful things?"

This opens a good conversation about ethics, technology, and responsibility - and whether the environmental problem is AI itself, or our lifestyle around it.

Did you know that...

The energy consumption of AI training is measured in "GPU-years". One GPU-year means a powerful GPU computes non-stop for a full year. Some models use thousands of GPU-years - but the efficiency of AI chips improves 2x per year, faster than Moore's Law.

Researchers are working on "tiny AI models" that are thousands of times smaller than large models. Some run entirely on a microcontroller using just a few milliwatts - which becomes particularly interesting for generative art and interactive installations.

Some new server racks are submerged in cooling liquid. This can reduce cooling energy by up to 90%. The most modern data centers achieve a PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness) of nearly 1.1 - almost all energy goes to the computations themselves.

Sources

The figures in this chapter are based on these research reports and analyses:

  • IEA - Energy and AI (2025) - Global IEA report. Data centres used ~415 TWh in 2024 (1.5% of global electricity); projected to double by 2030.
  • Alex de Vries - "The growing energy footprint of artificial intelligence" (Joule, 2023) - Academic analysis of AI electricity consumption. Estimated 564 MWh per day for ChatGPT.
  • Carbon Brief - AI: Five charts that put data-centre energy use into context - Clear comparison and nuance, with charts.
  • EU Covenant of Mayors - Stockholm: heat recovery from data centres - Concrete example: data centre heat warms 10,000+ homes in Stockholm.
  • BBC Future - The city where the internet warms people's homes - Background on Scandinavian district heating from server waste heat.

Tip for students: compare figures across different sources. Energy estimates for AI vary widely depending on the model, hardware and method. Be critical of absolute numbers.

Class Discussion

Good questions to discuss with students:

  • Is the environmental problem AI - or our lifestyle around it?
  • Streaming video, meat consumption, cars, and coffee often turn out to be bigger energy costs than digital interaction. How does that work?
  • If AI saves energy in other sectors, how do you add that up?
  • Who is responsible for the ecological cost of technology - the maker or the user?
