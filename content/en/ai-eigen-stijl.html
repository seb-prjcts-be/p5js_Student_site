AI can generate beautiful things - but they only become yours when they carry your handwriting. How do you guide AI so the output matches your aesthetics? And how do you keep your own voice in a world full of generated content?

The problem of generic output

When you ask an AI to "make a beautiful p5.js sketch", you get something that looks good - but it looks like what everyone gets. The AI defaults to the most predictable aesthetic, because that's what appears most often in the training data.

Your creative voice is the difference. The challenge is to use AI as an amplifier of your own ideas, not as a replacement.

Providing style references

The most powerful way to guide AI towards your style is by providing references in your prompt. Describe not only what you want, but also how it should look.

Example: vague prompt

"Make a generative art sketch in p5.js."

Result: probably circles, random colours, standard canvas.

Example: targeted prompt with style

"Make a p5.js sketch in the style of Vera Molnar:
geometric grids with subtle disruptions.
Use only black, white and one accent colour.
The shapes should be almost-regular,
with small random deviations that make it
feel human."

Result: much closer to your vision, because you've given the AI concrete aesthetic boundaries.

Iterative refinement

Rarely is the first output perfect. The real creative work is in the iteration - the back and forth between you and the AI.

The iteration process

  1. Version 1 - Describe your idea and let the AI generate a first version
  2. Evaluate - What's right? What's not? What's missing?
  3. Version 2 - Give targeted feedback: "The colours are too bright, make them softer. The movement is too fast."
  4. Version 3 - Refine further: "Add more variation in size. Let the shapes slowly rotate."

After three to five iterations you'll have something much closer to your vision than the first attempt.

Tip: save every version

Copy the code from each iteration before moving on. This way you can compare how your work has evolved and potentially go back to an earlier version.

Building your creative vocabulary

The better you can describe what you want, the better the AI can help you. Expand your aesthetic vocabulary:

  • Colour: warm, cool, pastel, saturated, monochrome, complementary
  • Composition: symmetrical, organic, grid-like, chaotic, minimalist
  • Movement: fluid, staccato, pulsing, drifting, explosive
  • Mood: calming, playful, ominous, dreamy, industrial
  • References: "like Mondrian", "in the style of pixel art", "brutalist"

Saving your style in the AI

Describing how your work should look every single time is tiring. Most AI tools have built-in features to save your preferences, so your style is automatically taken into account.

Custom Instructions (ChatGPT)

In ChatGPT you can set two things under Settings > Personalization > Custom instructions:

  • What should ChatGPT know about you? - e.g. "I'm an art student. I work with p5.js and like minimalist, geometric compositions."
  • How should ChatGPT respond? - e.g. "Write code with Dutch comments. Use a limited colour palette. Avoid unnecessary complexity."

This then applies to every new conversation.

Projects (Claude)

In Claude you can create Projects with custom instructions and files. You can:

  • Upload a style guide with your preferences (colours, shapes, mood)
  • Include examples of your own code as reference
  • Write instructions that apply to every conversation in that project

This way you don't have to explain who you are and what you want every time.

Copilot and Gemini

Copilot remembers preferences via Notebook mode and previously given instructions. Gemini has a similar memory via Gems - pre-configured personas that you can define yourself.

Example: style instruction for p5.js

You can set this as a custom instruction or project instruction:

Ik maak generatieve kunst met p5.js.
Mijn stijl:
- Beperkt kleurenpalet (max 4-5 kleuren)
- Geometrische vormen met subtiele organische variatie
- Rustige, langzame animaties
- Canvas altijd 600x400, zwarte of donkere achtergrond
- Nederlandse commentaren in de code
- Gebruik noise() in plaats van random() voor vloeiendheid

With an instruction like this, the AI immediately generates code that is closer to your aesthetic, without you having to repeat it every time.

Custom GPTs and skills

You can go one step further: build a fully reusable style profile that you can use again and again.

  • Custom GPTs (ChatGPT) - create your own GPT with fixed instructions, reference files and a specific personality. E.g. a "p5.js coach" that always generates in your style.
  • Skills (Claude Code) - reusable commands that apply a house style. E.g. a skill that says: "Apply the Lab44 house style" and automatically uses the right colours, fonts and layout.

The idea is the same: you define your style rules once, and the AI applies them every time. This way you build a consistent personal brand, even when the AI does the technical work.

Your own work as reference

You can also upload your own work as an example. If you have earlier sketches that you like, include them. The AI learns to recognise patterns: which functions you use, how you name variables, which colours you choose. The more examples, the better the AI can mimic your style.

Finding the balance

AI is a tool, not an author. The most interesting creative works emerge when you:

  • Use AI for the technical execution of your ideas
  • Make conscious choices about what you do and don't adopt
  • Modify, combine and transform the generated output
  • Can explain why you made certain choices

The difference between "AI made this" and "I made this with AI" lies in the intention and the iteration.
