Vera Molnar (1924-2023) was a French artist of Hungarian origin and one of the founders of digital and generative art. She worked with rules, systems, and minimal variation to create surprising visual results.

"I have no regrets. My life is squares, triangles, lines."

Biography: from brush to punch card

Molnar studied painting, art history, and aesthetics in Budapest (1942-1947). Early on she became fascinated by rules and systems, and was frustrated by the subjectivity of traditional art.

In 1947 she moved to Paris. There she resolutely chose abstract geometry: the grid and the rectangle became her basic motifs. She let go of nature as direct inspiration and sought a strict, almost mathematical visual language.

Machine Imaginaire and the transition to computers

Before she had access to computers, Molnar already worked algorithmically. In her "Machine Imaginaire" she wrote out rules and executed them step by step by hand. The goal was art driven not by intuition, but by logic and repetition.

From 1968 she gained access to a mainframe computer in Paris. She programmed on punch cards and waited for a plotter to put the drawings on paper. This made her one of the first artists to deliberately use the computer as an artistic medium.

Important themes and series

The search for 1% disorder

Molnar often started from a perfect grid and then introduced a minimal disturbance: a small shift, rotation, or deviation. This created tension between order and chaos.

Lettres de ma mere

In this series she translated her mother's handwriting into algorithms. The computer generated new, abstract letters and showed how data can also carry emotion.

Homages to the masters

Molnar analyzed compositions by artists such as Albrecht Durer and Paul Klee and reworked them into geometric and algorithmic structures, not as copies but as research.

Key concepts

  • Algorithmic art: art based on predetermined rules.
  • Iteration: repeating a shape with small variations.
  • Plotter art: physical drawings made by a machine with a pen.
  • Humanizing the machine: technology as a carrier of poetry and emotion.

How does it work?

The algorithm behind (Des)Ordres is surprisingly simple:

  1. Divide the canvas into a grid with a nested loop (row and column)
  2. Draw multiple concentric squares per cell (from outside to inside)
  3. Shift each corner with a small random() deviation
  4. The deeper the square sits inside the cell, the larger the deviation can be

The result is a tension between the perfect order of the grid and the human irregularity of the disturbances. Exactly that 1% chaos Molnar was looking for. See the working code example on the Loops page.
