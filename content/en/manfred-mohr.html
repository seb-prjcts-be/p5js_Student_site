Manfred Mohr (1938-2023) was one of the first artists to use a computer as a creative instrument. Where Vera Molnar started with manual instructions, Mohr engaged in dialogue with the machine from the very beginning - first as a jazz musician, later as an algorithmic artist.

From jazz to algorithms

Mohr grew up in Pforzheim (Germany) and started out as an abstract painter and action painter. In the 1960s he played alto saxophone in a jazz ensemble - a discipline that places improvisation within rules at its core.

That balance between structure and freedom would define his entire body of work. In 1969 he began writing code for a plotter at the Centre de Calcul of the University of Paris. He was one of the first in Europe to exhibit algorithmic art in a major museum: the Musee d'Art Moderne de la Ville de Paris in 1971.

The cube as a starting point

From the mid-1970s onward, Mohr focused almost exclusively on the hypercube - the four-dimensional extension of a cube. Not as a mathematical curiosity, but as an inexhaustible source of visual relationships.

His method:

  1. Take a cube (or hypercube) as an abstract model
  2. Write an algorithm that selects certain lines, planes, or angles
  3. Let the computer calculate all possible variations
  4. Choose from the results the compositions that are visually strongest

Each series of work is numbered with a P-code (P-xxxx). Together they form a systematic exploration of form spanning more than fifty years.

Important works and series

P-18 "Band" (1969)

One of his earliest plotter works. Horizontal lines are systematically disrupted by an algorithm. Simple rules produce complex texture - the core principle that runs through his entire body of work.

P-197 to P-200 "Cubic Limit" (1973-1977)

The breakthrough. Mohr reduces the cube to individual line segments and lets the algorithm decide which combinations appear on paper. The result: abstract compositions that are both mathematically rigid and visually surprising.

P-480 "Klangfarben" (1998-2003)

Color enters the picture. The name refers to Arnold Schoenberg's concept of "Klangfarbenmelodie" - color as a structural element, not as decoration. Mohr applies the same algorithmic logic: color is calculated, not chosen.

P-1414 "artificiata II" (2012-2015)

Later works based on 11-dimensional hypercubes. The complexity of the source increases, but the visual output remains clear and readable - proof of Mohr's skill in selecting rules that resonate.

What you can learn from Mohr

  • Limit your vocabulary - Mohr worked for decades with the same geometric basis. Limitation leads to depth.
  • Let the algorithm explore - write rules and see what comes out. Not every output is an artwork, but the selection is.
  • Structure is not a prison - just like in jazz, the most interesting art emerges when you find room to play within a system.
  • Numbering = discipline - Mohr's P-numbering forces you to document work and consciously build up series.
