A Lissajous figure is created when you combine two oscillations: one movement in x and one movement in y. The ratio between those oscillations determines the shape.

Why this is interesting

Here you can see that an image can actually be frozen motion. A shape isn't just drawn — it emerges from two rhythms running simultaneously.

Think about it: when does a shape feel harmonious, and when does it feel tense or chaotic?

What you'll learn here

  • using sine and periodicity visually
  • working with a parameter t that moves through time
  • the effect of ratios like 1:1, 2:1, or 3:2
  • how small phase shifts make a figure morph

The formula

Two equations, one parameter t running from 0 to 2pi:

x = A * sin(a * t + delta)
y = B * sin(b * t)
  • a and b - frequencies; their ratio determines the shape
  • delta - phase shift; makes the figure change
  • A and B - amplitudes, the size in x and y

Frequency ratios

  • 1:1 - circle or ellipse
  • 2:1 - simple loop or figure-8
  • 3:2 - braided pattern
  • 5:4 - dense, knot-like shape

That's why this topic is also a beautiful bridge between image, mathematics, and music.

In p5.js

You draw the curve by calculating a point for each value of t and connecting them with vertex():

beginShape();
for (let t = 0; t < TWO_PI; t += 0.01) {
  let x = width / 2 + 200 * sin(a * t + delta);
  let y = height / 2 + 160 * sin(b * t);
  vertex(x, y);
}
endShape();

By slowly increasing delta with frameCount, the phase shifts. This way you see the figure move through different states.
