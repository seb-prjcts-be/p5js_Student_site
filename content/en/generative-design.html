Generative design, or creative coding, is making art and visual patterns with the help of algorithms and code. It is not about AI-generated images, but about designing systems yourself that produce imagery, form, and variation. This form of creativity has a rich history going back to the 1960s.

The Origins

Generative design began with pioneers like John Whitney, who in the 1960s experimented with computer animation and mathematical patterns. He used analog computers to create abstract animations that laid the foundation for what we now call creative coding.

Key Moments

  • 1960s: John Whitney creates the first computer-generated animations
  • 1970s: Fractals are discovered and used in art (Mandelbrot set)
  • 1980s: The DEMO scene emerges — programmers create visual art with code
  • 2000s: Processing is developed, making creative coding accessible
  • 2010s: p5.js brings Processing to the web

What is Generative Design?

Generative Design is a fusion of art and technology, where you use algorithms and code to create visual compositions. You don't draw image by image, but instead design a system that produces images.

Unlike traditional art, where you determine every detail yourself, here you define rules, parameters, and variation. Those rules produce the work: you no longer make a single poster, but a system to generate thousands of unique posters.

By allowing repetition, chance, and controlled disruption, patterns emerge that you could never have drawn one by one. That is precisely the power of generative art: you design the process, the computer explores the possibilities.

Advantages

Generative Design combines creativity with systems thinking. This yields several clear advantages:

  • You create many variations in a short time and discover unexpected solutions.
  • Mathematical patterns are precise and repeatable, allowing you to experiment in a targeted way.
  • Interaction and animation feel natural because the system responds to time and input.
  • You learn to program in a creative way and build your own tools.

Examples from History

Fractals

In the early 1980s, mathematician Benoit Mandelbrot brought the concepts of fractals to the forefront. Fractals are patterns that repeat themselves at different scales. Well-known examples include the Mandelbrot set and the Koch snowflake.

Fractals show how simple formulas can form complex structures. They immediately made generative art visible to a broad audience.

DEMO scene

The demoscene is an international subculture of computer art that produces demos: small programs that generate audiovisual presentations. It's all about programming, visuals, music, and showmanship.

The annual DEMO festival (Design in Motion) claims public advertising screens as exhibition space and shows moving, colorful creations in the city.

BYOB (Bring Your Own Beamer)

Bring Your Own Beamer is a format where artists show their work on their own projector. Many cities have a local version, and in Ghent, Nerdlab is a well-known organizer.

The result is a temporary exhibition where generative images, animations, and live visuals come together in a shared space.
