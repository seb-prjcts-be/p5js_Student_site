Generative Design is a fascinating fusion of art and technology, where algorithms and code are used to create visual compositions. This art form has its roots in the early days of computer art and remains a relevant and evolving field. One of the pioneers of this movement is John Whitney, whose work laid the foundation for modern generative art.

The early years

The roots of generative design can be traced back to the 1960s and 70s, when computers were still in their infancy. Artists like Vera Molnár and Manfred Mohr began experimenting with plotter drawings, using algorithms to generate abstract forms.

John Whitney and the beginnings of Generative Design

John Whitney was an American animator, composer, and pioneer in the field of computer animation. He was one of the first to see the potential of computers not only to solve scientific problems, but also to produce aesthetic and artistic creations. His experiments with computer graphics in the 1960s and 70s paved the way for today's generative artists.

Key moments

  • 1960s: John Whitney creates the first computer-generated animations
  • 1960-70s: Pioneers like Vera Molnár and Manfred Mohr use plotters and algorithms to create art
  • 1970s: Fractals are discovered and used in art (Mandelbrot set)
  • 1980s: The DEMO scene emerges - programmers create visual art with code
  • 2000s: Processing is developed by Casey Reas and Ben Fry, making creative coding accessible
  • 2010s: p5.js brings Processing to the web; live coding and Algorave become a movement

Fractals

In the early 1980s, mathematician Benoît Mandelbrot brought the concepts of fractals to the forefront, which had an enormous impact on generative art.

Be sure to watch the documentary Clouds are not Spheres on Netflix.

The rise of Processing and p5.js

In the early 21st century, programming languages and libraries emerged such as Processing, founded by Casey Reas and Ben Fry, and later p5.js, developed by Lauren McCarthy.

These languages made it easier for artists, designers and students to code, and opened the door to a new generation of Creative Coding.

What makes Generative Design / Creative Coding so unique?

Unlike traditional art forms, where the artist has complete control over every aspect of the work, generative art is about creating a system or a set of rules that produces the art. This can range from simple patterns and textures to complex fractals and dynamic animations. You no longer make a single poster, but a system to generate thousands of unique posters!

Advantages

Generative Design combines creativity with systems thinking. This yields several clear advantages:

  • You create many variations in a short time and discover unexpected solutions.
  • Mathematical patterns are precise and repeatable, allowing you to experiment in a targeted way.
  • Interaction and animation feel natural because the system responds to time and input.
  • You learn to program in a creative way and build your own tools.

Live coding at the algorave

Live coding is a fascinating subculture within the world of Creative Coding.

In performances, artists code in real time to generate visual or auditory experiences, often accompanied by live music. The artist's screen is usually projected for the audience, so viewers can follow the creative process from start to finish.

This adds a layer of transparency and interactivity that is rarely seen in other art forms. Well-known platforms for live coding include TidalCycles for music and Hydra for visual art. This course also covers Strudel, a modern evolution of TidalCycles in the browser. Events like Algoraves combine live coding with rave culture, where artists and audience together create an immersive, collaborative experience.

The phenomenon of live coding challenges traditional notions of performance and art, and offers a compelling intersection of technology, creativity and community-building.

Bring Your Own Beamer

At Bring Your Own Beamer events, digital artists show their work on their own beamers. Almost every major city has a local version of BYOB.

In Ghent, it is mainly Nerdlab that is the driving force behind this event.

DEMO festival

The annual DEMO festival (Design in Motion) is a recurring project that is quietly taking the world by storm. For one day, all public advertising screens in the streetscape are claimed as exhibition space for Creative Coders. DEMO fills every city with colourful, surprising and moving creations.

The driving force behind DEMO is Tim Rodenbröker.

The demoscene is an international subculture of computer art focused on producing demos: self-contained, sometimes extremely small computer programs that produce audiovisual presentations. The goal of a demo is to show off programming, visual art and musical skills. Demos and other demoscene productions (graphics, music, videos, games) are shared at festivals known as "demo parties", where attendees vote and productions are released online. The scene began with the home computer revolution in the early 1980s.

Other projects

Listen to Wikipedia

Listen to the sound of recent changes on Wikipedia. Bells indicate additions; a plucked string means someone removed something. The pitch depends on the size of the edit: the larger the edit, the lower the note. By Hatnote.

MTA.ME

On mta.me, Conductor turns the New York subway system into an interactive string instrument. Using the actual MTA subway schedule, the piece starts in real time by releasing trains that departed in the last minute, then continues to accelerate over a 24-hour loop. By Alexander Chen.

Land Lines

Land Lines is an experiment that lets you explore Google Earth satellite imagery through gestures. Draw something to find satellite images that match each line; drag longer to create an infinite line of connected rivers, highways and coastlines. Thanks to a combination of machine learning, optimised algorithms and a strong graphics card, the experiment runs efficiently in the web browser. By Zach Lieberman and Matt Felsen.

Audience (2008)

An army of mirrors mounted on small metal feet are clearly mechanical, yet the way they move displays almost human-like behaviour. Through simple turning and tilting movements, similar to turning the head, the mirrors convey individual characters, but when a person approaches, they all turn toward the visitor in a single curious, synchronised gesture. Reflected in each mirror, the viewer becomes the subject of both their own gaze and the artwork's. By Random International.

Everything in Existence

Everything in Existence was the first solo exhibition of fuse* in the United States, which took place in Washington DC from 17 January to 10 March 2019. It featured some of the studio's most important works to date, such as Multiverse and Amygdala, and was inspired by the idea of interconnectedness - the realisation that we are all part of everything that exists.

XYZT: Abstract Landscapes

XYZT, created by the company Adrien M & Claire B, explores the intersection of mathematics and imaginary landscapes.

Using technology, programming and lighting design, XYZT lets visitors explore the four primary axes of existence: horizontal (the X-axis), vertical (Y), depth (Z) and time (T). The exhibition offers unparalleled interactivity on each plane, responds to the movement of visitors, and creates new imagery in real time.

Technicontrol

In his creative coding work Tecnicontrol, Bradley Munkowitz - also known in the art community as GMUNK - explores how robotics, code and screen content can result in a choreographed piece. Instead of using the typical projection canvases, he insisted on robots with LED screens and a motion-controlled camera. The end result is a whimsical, technology-driven video with a truly stunning storyline.

Conclusion

Generative Design is not only an art form but also an exploration of the possibilities and implications of technology. The work of the great pioneers remains a source of inspiration and a foundation for today's artists and designers. In a world increasingly driven by technology, Generative Design remains a relevant and challenging domain that pushes the boundaries of creativity and programming.
