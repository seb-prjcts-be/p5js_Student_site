Generative design, or creative coding, is making art and visual patterns with the help of algorithms and code. It is not about AI-generated images, but about designing systems yourself that produce imagery, form, and variation. This form of creativity has a rich history going back to the 1960s.

The Origins

Generative design began with pioneers like John Whitney, who in the 1960s experimented with computer animation and mathematical patterns. He was an American animator, composer, and pioneer in computer animation. He was one of the first to see the potential of computers not only to solve scientific problems but also to generate aesthetic and artistic creations.

Artists such as Vera Molnar and Manfred Mohr began experimenting with plotter drawings during the same period, using algorithms to generate abstract forms.

Key Moments

  • 1960s: John Whitney creates the first computer-generated animations
  • 1970s: Fractals are discovered and used in art (Mandelbrot set)
  • 1980s: The DEMO scene emerges - programmers create visual art with code
  • 2000s: Processing is developed, making creative coding accessible
  • 2010s: p5.js brings Processing to the web

What is Generative Design?

Generative Design is a fusion of art and technology, where you use algorithms and code to create visual compositions. You don't draw image by image, but instead design a system that produces images.

Unlike traditional art, where you determine every detail yourself, here you define rules, parameters, and variation. Those rules produce the work: you no longer make a single poster, but a system to generate thousands of unique posters.

By allowing repetition, chance, and controlled disruption, patterns emerge that you could never have drawn one by one. That is precisely the power of generative art: you design the process, the computer explores the possibilities.

Advantages

Generative Design combines creativity with systems thinking. This yields several clear advantages:

  • You create many variations in a short time and discover unexpected solutions.
  • Mathematical patterns are precise and repeatable, allowing you to experiment in a targeted way.
  • Interaction and animation feel natural because the system responds to time and input.
  • You learn to program in a creative way and build your own tools.

Examples from History

Fractals

In the early 1980s, mathematician Benoit Mandelbrot brought the concepts of fractals to the forefront. Fractals are patterns that repeat themselves at different scales. Well-known examples include the Mandelbrot set and the Koch snowflake.

Fractals show how simple formulas can form complex structures. They immediately made generative art visible to a broad audience.

Live coding and the algorave

Live coding is a fascinating subculture within creative coding. In performances, artists code in real-time to generate visual or auditory experiences, often accompanied by live music. The artist's screen is usually projected for the audience, so viewers can follow the creative process from start to finish.

Well-known platforms for live coding include TidalCycles for music and Hydra for visual art. Events like Algoraves combine live coding with rave culture, creating an immersive experience for artists and audience alike. Also check the Strudel page for live coding music in the browser.

DEMO scene

The demoscene is an international subculture of computer art focused on producing demos: self-contained, sometimes extremely small computer programs that produce audiovisual presentations. The goal is to showcase programming, visual art, and musical skills. The scene began with the home computer revolution in the early 1980s.

The annual DEMO festival (Design in Motion), founded by Tim Rodenbroker, claims public advertising screens as exhibition space and shows moving, colorful creations in the city.

BYOB (Bring Your Own Beamer)

Bring Your Own Beamer is a format where artists show their work on their own projector. Many cities have a local version, and in Ghent, Nerdlab is a well-known organizer.

The result is a temporary exhibition where generative images, animations, and live visuals come together in a shared space.

Contemporary projects

Generative design and creative coding thrive today in many diverse forms. Some notable projects:

  • Scan Sequence (Jeongho Park) - a webcam scans objects and converts them to sound. Image becomes sound.
  • Listen to Wikipedia (Hatnote) - every edit on Wikipedia is translated into a tone. Thousands of edits per minute become a continuous soundscape.
  • Land Lines (Zach Lieberman & Matt Felsen) - draw a gesture and the algorithm finds a satellite photo containing that same shape. Google Earth as a creative medium.
  • Audience (Random International, 2008) - an installation with hundreds of small mirrors that turn toward the visitor. Technology that observes you while you observe it.
  • XYZT: Abstract Landscapes (Adrien M & Claire B) - mathematical landscapes where X, Y, Z describe space and T describes time. Visitors move through abstract particle clouds.
