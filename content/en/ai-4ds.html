AI is not smart. You are - if you use it well. The 4D's are four steps to consciously use AI as a learning partner, not as an answer machine. This framework was developed by Lab44 at Kunsthumaniora Sint-Lucas Gent.

AI as a learning partner, not a shortcut

You already use AI - or you will. The question is not whether, but how. The difference:

What doesn't work

  • Letting AI write what you submit
  • Copying answers without understanding
  • Using AI to avoid thinking

What does work

  • Having AI explain what you don't understand yet
  • Having AI quiz and challenge you
  • Asking AI for hints, not solutions
  • Keeping track of what you learned yourself - without AI, and thanks to AI

The 4D's

The 4D's are four steps that help you use AI consciously: Detect what you already know and can do, Define what you want to learn, Do with AI as your guide, and Document so you can see how far you've come.

D1: Detect

Who are you as a learner?

Before you can use AI meaningfully, you need to know who you are. What are you good at? Where do you get stuck? When do you need help, and when would you rather figure it out yourself?

Ask yourself these questions

  • About your subjects: Which subjects go well? Where do you get stuck the quickest? What kind of assignments do you enjoy most?
  • About how you learn: Do you learn better by seeing, hearing, or doing? When do you tune out? What motivates you?
  • About AI: Have you used AI before? How? What do you find useful about it? When do you not want to use it?

Be honest - nobody is grading this. Be specific - "math" is too vague. Think about when you don't want AI.

D2: Define

Set your own rules.

You decide how AI helps you. Not the other way around. That means: thinking about what you want to learn and deciding when AI is welcome - and when it's not.

Create a learning context document

Write a short personal document that describes:

  • Your strengths and weaknesses as a learner
  • How you learn best
  • When you want to use AI
  • Your boundaries - what AI should not do

Open this document at the start of every new AI conversation. That way AI knows who you are and what you need, and you don't start from zero every time.

Set up study protocols

Create rules for yourself about how you use AI in different situations:

  • "If I don't understand something - explain it and test whether I get it now"
  • "If I'm working on an assignment - hints, not solutions"
  • "If I'm studying for a test - quiz me"
  • "If I'm writing - give feedback on my text"

D3: Do

AI as your guide, you in the lead.

This is the heart of the approach. You do the work, AI guides you. The difference is in how you ask the question.

The starting prompt

Start every AI conversation with something like:

"Ik wil dat je mijn studiebuddy bent. Help me leren,
maar doe mijn werk niet voor me. Stel me vragen in
plaats van meteen antwoorden te geven. Check of ik
iets écht begrijp voor je verdergaat.
Hier is mijn leercontext: [document]"

Protocols in practice

  • Learning a concept: "I don't understand X. Explain it with an example. Then ask me three questions to check if I really get it."
  • Working on an assignment: "I'm working on Y. Give me hints and questions, but don't write or solve anything for me. I'll do it myself."
  • Practicing for a test: "Quiz me on Z. If I give a wrong answer, explain why it's wrong and why the correct answer is correct."

The test after every conversation

"Can I explain this now without AI?" If the answer is no, you haven't learned yet - you've just finished something. That's a difference that matters.

D4: Document

Keep track of how far you've come.

Learning is invisible until you make it visible. A learning journal helps you follow your own growth.

Weekly (5 minutes)

  • What did I work on this week?
  • What do I understand better now?
  • What is still unclear or difficult?
  • How did I use AI - for learning or for escaping?
  • What do I want to understand better next week?
  • + One aha moment

Monthly reflection

  • What did I really learn on my own (without AI)?
  • What did I learn thanks to AI?
  • Do you see a pattern in what you find difficult?
  • What will I do differently next month?

The journal is for you. Not for a grade, not for your teacher. The more honest you are, the more you'll get out of it.

4 things every student should know

1. AI hallucinates

AI makes up facts, names, dates, or sources that don't exist - but writes them as if they're perfectly true. This is called a hallucination. AI doesn't have access to the truth: it picks the most plausible word, and sometimes that's just wrong.

What do you do? Always check facts through another source. Challenge AI: "Are you sure about this?"

2. Promptcraft

How you ask determines what you get. A vague question gives a vague answer. A good prompt contains four elements:

  1. Role - "I'm a 16-year-old student..."
  2. Goal - "I don't understand X..."
  3. Format - "Use an analogy..."
  4. Action - "Ask me 2 questions..."

3. Honesty and integrity

Submitting AI output as your own work is cheating. The gray area is in the middle:

  • Asking AI to explain something - ok
  • Using AI to improve your own text - ok
  • Asking AI for feedback on your work - ok
  • Having AI write, then editing it - gray area
  • Copying AI text without credit - not ok
  • Having AI fill in answers on tests - not ok

The simple test: "Can I explain this to someone now without AI?"

4. Privacy

What you tell AI leaves your computer. Forever. Never share:

  • Your full name + school
  • Address or location
  • Photos or personal info
  • Things about classmates
  • Passwords or codes

You don't actually need your real name. Just write "I" or make up a nickname.

Get started

Start today:

  1. Write your learning context document (D1 + D2)
  2. Open an AI tool and paste your document as a starting point
  3. Use a study protocol for your first question (D3)
  4. Afterwards, write 3 sentences: what did I learn? (D4)
